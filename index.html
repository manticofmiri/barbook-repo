01 · Problem Space

Going out should be easy

Choosing where to eat with a group is often a frustrating, multi-platform experience. Google Maps lacks dietary filters. Reservation apps don't show menus. Social media is not searchable by need.

"I'm the one who always has to suggest places — and I never know which ones are vegan-friendly."

People who eat out frequently — especially those with dietary restrictions or in mixed groups — have no reliable, unified tool that combines discovery, filtering, and booking in one place.

3+
apps users open to find and book a table for a group
68%
of users abandon a platform when dietary filters are missing
  • No single platform combines search, filters, menu preview, and reservations
  • Vegan and vegetarian users consistently report exclusion from mainstream discovery apps
  • Group organizers feel the pressure of always having to find a "safe" venue for everyone
  • People lose time calling restaurants to confirm availability and dietary options
  • Saved lists and favourites are scattered across different apps with no unified record
02 · Research

Understanding real users

I conducted qualitative research with 8 participants across different dining profiles to uncover the pain points, motivations, and mental models around finding and visiting restaurants.

User Interviews Contextual Inquiry Competitive Benchmarking Affinity Mapping Usability Testing
🗣

User Interviews

8 semi-structured interviews exploring habits, frustrations, and expectations around dining out and restaurant discovery. Participants ranged from solo diners to group organizers with specific dietary needs.

🔍

Competitive Benchmarking

Analysed Google Maps, TripAdvisor, OpenTable, and local apps across 14 key criteria: filter depth, dietary options, reservation flow, saved lists, and social features.

🗂

Affinity Mapping

Clustered over 60 research insights into themes that shaped the core feature set: dietary discovery, group coordination, trust signals, and booking simplicity.

Key research insights

  • Users trust platforms that show real photos, opening hours, and confirmed menus together
  • Vegan and vegetarian users have a strong need to pre-filter before even seeing options
  • Outdoor seating is a primary differentiator that no app surfaces well
  • Group organisers want to share options and confirm bookings without leaving the app
  • Users want to revisit saved places and easily book again without re-searching

08 · Usability Testing

Validating with real users

Moderated usability sessions were conducted with 5 participants using a clickable Figma prototype. The test was built around a realistic scenario.

Test Scenario

Your birthday is coming up and you want to celebrate with friends at an outdoor café. Many of your friends are vegan. Find a venue, reserve a table for 8 people, and save it to your favourites.

  • T1Search, filter, and reserve a venue for 8 people for a birthday tea-time
  • T2Log in to the app
  • T3Save a gastronomic venue to favourites
  • T4View your existing reservations

Success Criteria

Users must arrive at the reservation confirmation screen and demonstrate they understand the booking has been made.

09 · Learnings

What I learned

This project sharpened my understanding of inclusive design, the tension between discoverability and simplicity, and the value of testing assumptions early.

01

Dietary needs are primary, not secondary

Placing dietary filters front and centre — not buried in advanced search — changed the entire emotional experience for users like Claudia. Inclusive design means making it the default, not an add-on.

02

Users read icons slower than labels

Navigation icons without labels caused hesitation during testing. A hybrid label + icon approach on the bottom bar eliminated confusion and sped up task completion significantly.

03

Confirmation = Trust

A clear, celebratory confirmation screen after booking was the most impactful single screen. Users repeatedly mentioned it as the moment they "trusted" the app. Feedback loops build confidence.

04

Filtering must be fast

Every additional tap to apply a filter caused drop-off intention in interview participants. Chips on the search screen — not in a modal — reduced the filter flow from 4 taps to 1.

05

Test with the real scenario

Using a realistic birthday scenario rather than abstract tasks gave richer insights. Users were emotionally invested, which revealed anxiety points that abstract tasks would have missed.

06

Saved lists drive return visits

Users who saved a venue during the test session immediately wanted to book it again. The favourites feature creates a retention loop — a business insight that emerged purely from observation.

10 · Next Steps

Where this goes next

The validated MVP sets a clear foundation. These are the prioritised next phases to move from prototype to a shippable product.

01
Phase 1 · Immediate

Iterate on usability findings

Increase contrast on the Save icon, add labels to bottom navigation, and surface party-size as a top-level filter chip. Re-test with the same 5 participants.

02
Phase 2 · Short-term

Expand filter taxonomy

Add gluten-free, halal, and kosher dietary filters. Introduce price range and ambiance filters (quiet, lively, date-night). Build out the venue profile with full menu integration.

03
Phase 3 · Medium-term

Social & group features

Allow users to share venue options with friends before booking. Add a group voting feature and collaborative saved lists. Introduce push notifications for reservation reminders.
